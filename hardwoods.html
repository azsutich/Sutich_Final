What Are Hardwoods?

Hardwoods come from deciduous trees—those that lose their leaves annually. These trees grow more slowly than softwoods, resulting in denser, tighter grain structures that offer greater strength and durability. This makes hardwoods ideal for applications like furniture, flooring, cabinetry, and instruments. They’re typically more expensive and harder to work with than softwoods, but their resilience and beauty make them worth the effort.

Common Domestic Hardwood Species

Oak

  • Hardness: 1,290 lbf (Red Oak)
  • Best Uses: Flooring, furniture, cabinetry
  • Notable Info: Prominent grain, readily available, takes stain well

Maple

  • Hardness: 1,450 lbf (Hard Maple)
  • Best Uses: Cutting boards, dressers, musical instruments
  • Notable Info: Very durable and smooth; difficult to stain evenly

Cherry

  • Hardness: 950 lbf
  • Best Uses: Cabinets, fine furniture, paneling
  • Notable Info: Darkens beautifully with age and exposure to light

Walnut

  • Hardness: 1,010 lbf
  • Best Uses: Carving, gunstocks, high-end furniture
  • Notable Info: Naturally dark and rich, works well with hand tools

Common Exotic Hardwood Species

Mahogany

  • Hardness: ~800 lbf
  • Best Uses: Musical instruments, furniture, boats
  • Notable Info: Revered for its tonal properties and workability

Teak

  • Hardness: 1,070 lbf
  • Best Uses: Outdoor furniture, ship decking
  • Notable Info: Naturally weather-resistant due to its oil content

Ebony

  • Hardness: 3,220 lbf (varies by species)
  • Best Uses: Inlay, fine instruments, decorative pieces
  • Notable Info: Extremely dense and expensive, jet black appearance

Rosewood

  • Hardness: ~2,440 lbf
  • Best Uses: Guitars, veneers, luxury furniture
  • Notable Info: Visually striking with deep reds and browns

Sustainability

Due to slow growth rates and high demand, some hardwood species are endangered or overharvested. Look for wood certified by the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) or choose reclaimed lumber when possible. Domestic species like oak and maple are often more sustainable choices than exotic woods. Advances in responsible forestry and alternative materials are helping reduce the environmental impact of hardwood production.
